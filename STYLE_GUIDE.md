# The Corn — Style Guide

*The bit is: small-town hysteria is funny. The residents are not the joke — the **collective panic** is.*

---

## The Golden Rule

**Punch up at the phenomenon, never down at the people.**

We're parodying the *vibe* of hyperlocal Facebook groups — the breathless urgency, the conspiratorial undertones, the certainty that an unfamiliar car means organized crime has arrived. We are **not** parodying any actual human being who posted any actual thing.

If a draft makes a real identifiable person look stupid, it's a bad draft. Rewrite it.

---

## Names

**Never use:**
- Real first + last names from source posts
- Real initials (even "Brenda M." is too identifiable in a town of 10k)
- Real usernames, profile details, or anything traceable

**Use instead, in order of preference:**

1. **Generic descriptors** *(default choice)*
   - "a longtime resident"
   - "one local woman" / "a local man"
   - "a concerned homeowner"
   - "a community member"
   - "several residents"

2. **Bit-flavored descriptors** *(when it adds to the joke)*
   - "a man who asked not to be named because They listen"
   - "a woman who has lived in town since before the new subdivision"
   - "a resident who clarified she is 'not normally one to post'"

3. **Invented composite names** *(use sparingly, for variety)*
   - Pick names so common they belong to no one: Karen Doyle, Linda Pearson, Dave Thompson
   - Avoid any name that could match a known local figure
   - **Cross-check:** if you can think of a real person in town with that name, don't use it

**Hard no:** clever puns on real names, anagrams, "totally not [real person]" winks. If it points at someone, it's out.

---

## Businesses & Locations

**Never name:**
- Independent local businesses (Sue's Cafe, The Bookmark, etc.)
- Specific street addresses
- Named landmarks tied to one owner (someone's farm, someone's barn)
- Schools, churches, community centers by real name

**Safe to name:**
- National chains big enough to be cosmic background radiation: Tim Hortons, Foodland (chain), Canadian Tire, LCBO
- **Even then:** prefer "the Tim Hortons" over "the Tim Hortons on Queen Street"

**Use instead:**
- "a local grocery store"
- "the downtown coffee shop"
- "a Main Street business"
- Invented plausible names: Ridgewater Plaza, the Cedar Street IGA, Lakeshore Lanes

---

## Source Posts

- **We never reproduce a real post verbatim.** Always reword, recombine, exaggerate.
- **Composite > single source.** Blend 2–3 panicked posts on the same theme into one fictional inciting event. Harder to trace, funnier, more representative.
- **Shift the specifics.** Change dates, swap "white van" for "grey SUV," move it from behind Foodland to behind "a local grocery store." Keep the *energy*, lose the *fingerprint*.

---

## Tone

**Yes:**
- Deadpan AP-style voice contrasted against absurd subject matter
- Treating non-events with grave seriousness ("the most pressing crisis since…")
- Quoting "officials" who patiently explain that nothing is happening
- The phenomenon of vigilance itself as the subject

**No:**
- Mocking poor grammar/spelling from source posts
- Mocking older residents, working-class people, anyone for *who they are*
- Political punching (left or right) — keep it about the town, not the team
- Anything that reads as contempt rather than affection

The energy we want: *"This town is insane and I love it."*
Not: *"These people are idiots."*

---

## Sensitive Topics — Avoid

- Anything involving real children, schools, or named minors (even composites — just skip the topic)
- Real crimes, real accidents, real deaths (no parodying actual tragedy)
- Mental health, addiction, domestic situations — even when FB posts touch on them
- Anything race/ethnicity-coded from a "suspicious stranger" post — that's where the panic gets ugly, and we don't echo it
- Specific local political figures or candidates by name

When a source post wades into any of these, **drop that thread entirely** and find a different angle on the same general hysteria.

---

## The Sniff Test

Before publishing, ask:

1. Could a specific real person read this and recognize themselves as the butt of the joke? → **Rewrite**
2. Could a specific real business lose customers over this? → **Rewrite**
3. Is the joke "people in this town are stupid"? → **Rewrite** (the joke is "collective panic is funny")
4. Would I be comfortable if this person/business saw it? → If no, **rewrite**
5. Does this make me snort? → **Publish**
